Camelid Research Grants

Funding the Science That Keeps Herds Healthy

Since 1987, we have supported camelid health research at Oregon State University's Carlson College of Veterinary Medicine and, in recent years, at Washington State University College of Veterinary Medicine.

Submit a Research Proposal

We invite qualified veterinary staff, senior students, and PhD candidates at OSU and WSU to apply for research funding each year. All proceeds from our annual fundraiser events go directly to medical research and to the maintenance of the OSU research herd.

In addition to fundraiser proceeds, the Research Committee administers donations from supporting organizations and businesses. Because costs continue to rise and demand for funding exceeds what is available, the Committee carefully evaluates each proposal against our research priorities before selecting projects to fund.

"Education and research is why we exist."

— Glen Pfefferkorn, NWCF President

How to Apply

  1. Download and review the Research Guidelines to confirm your project qualifies.
  2. Complete the Research Proposal Application.
  3. Submit your application to the Research Committee by the annual deadline.
  4. The Committee will review all proposals and notify applicants of funding decisions.

Research Spotlight

Camelid Nanobodies: Small Molecules, Big Impact

Antibodies are proteins the body produces in response to foreign substances like bacteria, viruses, or tumor cells. In almost every animal species, antibodies have two components: heavy chains and light chains. In 1989, scientists discovered that camelids — llamas and alpacas among them — produce a unique antibody that is missing the light chain entirely.

These smaller, fully functional antibodies can be further reduced in the laboratory into structures called nanobodies. Because of their compact size, nanobodies can penetrate cells and reach locations that conventional antibodies cannot. They are also less likely to cause a harmful immune response when injected into a patient.

At OSU's Carlson College of Veterinary Medicine, Dr. Chris Cebra and his colleagues have been producing nanobodies by working with llamas and alpacas. Current targets include the bacteria that causes tuberculosis, the virus that causes Dengue Fever, and cancer proteins in dogs. This research is conducted in collaboration with Oregon Health Sciences University, Case Western Reserve, and other OSU colleges.

"I was contacted by the University of Georgia about a sick cria. They tried the dose rate from our NWCF study and saw a fourfold increase in white count in 48 hours — they were very happy."

— Dr. Erica McKenzie, BSc, BVMS, PhD, Diplomate ACVIM
Assistant Professor of Large Animal Medicine, Oregon State University

Fun Fact

The only other animal known to produce these "small" antibodies is the shark. It is logistically easier to work with llamas and alpacas.

— Dr. Susan Tornquist, Dean, Carlson College of Veterinary Medicine, Oregon State University
