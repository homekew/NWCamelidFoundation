Veterinary Scholarships

Investing in the Next Generation of Camelid Veterinarians

Each year the North West Camelid Foundation awards scholarships to veterinary students at Oregon State University and Washington State University who demonstrate a passion for camelid medicine and a commitment to advancing the health of llamas and alpacas.

About the Scholarships

NWCF scholarship recipients are selected by a committee at each veterinary college based on academic merit, demonstrated interest in camelid medicine, and financial need. The Foundation has awarded more than $36,000 in scholarships since the program began.

Scholarships are funded entirely by proceeds from our annual fundraiser events and by direct donations from individuals and supporting organizations. Every gift to NWCF has the potential to support a future camelid specialist.

"Regardless of the size of their herd or the location of their barn, every camelid and every owner benefits from the research and education funded by the NWCF."

— North West Camelid Foundation

Scholarship Program Details

  • Awards are made annually at Oregon State University and Washington State University
  • Selection is managed by the Scholarship Committee at each college
  • Eligibility is limited to enrolled DVM students in good academic standing
  • Preference is given to students with a demonstrated interest in large animal or camelid medicine
  • Award amounts vary by year depending on available funds

For Prospective Applicants

Applications are accepted through each college's financial aid or scholarship office. Students interested in the NWCF scholarship should contact their college's scholarship coordinator directly for application requirements and deadlines.

Why It Matters

Building a Camelid Specialty

Specialized veterinary care for llamas and alpacas is only possible when the next generation of veterinarians has been trained and supported. NWCF scholarships help make that happen.

Reduce Financial Barriers

Veterinary education is expensive. Scholarships allow students to pursue their interest in camelid medicine without being solely constrained by financial pressure.

Grow the Specialty

The more students who receive focused support and encouragement in camelid medicine, the more specialists will be available to serve owners and herds across the country.

Connect Research and Practice

Scholarship recipients often go on to careers that bridge clinical practice and research, translating lab discoveries into real-world outcomes for camelids.

History

How the Scholarship Endowment Began

In 1993, the NWCF Board of Directors approved funding an annual scholarship for a student pursuing a degree in veterinary medicine. A Scholarship Committee was formed under the leadership of Bob Wynia, who along with other volunteers developed the qualifying criteria, distributed award announcements, and selected recipients. From the beginning, demonstrated interest in the care and treatment of llamas and alpacas was included alongside scholastic achievement as a qualifying criterion.

In the early years, the scholarship was funded by a raffle held at the Llama Bazaar at AgFest at the State Fairgrounds each April. Participating farms donated pairs of pet male llamas, and ticket sales regularly exceeded $1,000. When raffle proceeds fell short, directors and members made up the difference from the treasury or through personal donations.

By 2001, the administrative complexity of maintaining the scholarship independently prompted the Board to pursue a more permanent solution. A challenge was issued to llama owners: for every dollar donated individually, NWCF would match with four dollars, up to a maximum matching grant of $10,000. The $11,000 windfall earned through the NWCF's first ALSA-sanctioned llama show in 1999 — under the leadership of youth superintendent Justin Timm — provided the matching funds.

On November 14, 2002, NWCF director Glen Pfeferkorn signed the Endowment Fund Agreement with the Oregon State University Foundation, formally establishing the NWCF Scholarship Endowment Fund in partnership with OSU's College of Veterinary Medicine. All administrative responsibility for the scholarship was transferred to the Scholarship Committee of the College of Veterinary Medicine. By March 31, 2003, the fund was fully funded.

The endowment also carries a memorial legacy. In September 2001, the Board transferred $382 from the Raven Hill Fund to the Scholarship endowment — a tribute to Jan Crabbe, who bred llamas in the Corvallis area under the name Raven Hill Llamas. When Jan passed away from cancer, her friends contributed to a memorial fund with NWCF. Her family agreed that the Scholarship Fund was a fitting place for that tribute to live on.

Lane's goal is a mobile, mixed-animal rural practice offering comprehensive care for barn cats, livestock guardian dogs, horses, pigs, sheep, alpacas, and cattle — and continuing the shift in large animal medicine toward humane practices.

2022  ·  Washington State University
